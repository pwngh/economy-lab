economy-lab is the application layer of a credits economy (wallets, a marketplace, payouts, subscriptions, and digital ownership) built as one library over a single [double-entry](/economy/concepts/accounts-and-double-entry/) ledger.

You drive it through one [`submit`](/economy/reference/the-economy/) entry point and read its state through [`read`](/economy/reference/reads/). Every action is one [`Operation`](/economy/reference/operations/) that posts a balanced transaction and returns an [`Outcome`](/economy/reference/outcomes-and-reason-codes/).

Balances aren't stored and mutated. Every action becomes a balanced posting in an append-only, hash-chained ledger, and balances are folded back from those postings.

The project enforces several [invariants](/economy/concepts/the-proof/): value is conserved, no account overdrafts, every spendable credit is backed by real cash, and history can't be silently rewritten. A [prover](/economy/concepts/the-proof/) re-checks all of them after every operation.

## How it fits together

A `submit` is synchronous. It validates and authorizes the request first. Then it posts one balanced transaction to the [ledger](/economy/concepts/accounts-and-double-entry/), writing a matching event to an outbox in the same transaction. The [Economy API](/economy/reference/the-economy/) walks that request path step by step.

Anything that outlives a request happens off that path. A [background worker](/economy/reference/background-worker/) drains the outbox and runs the recurring sweeps: advancing payouts, billing subscriptions, expiring promos, realizing fees, and sealing checkpoints.

## A lab, not a product

economy-lab is a runnable study of an application layer, not a deployable money system. The ledger invariants are enforced to a production standard: pushed down into the database, and proven by an adversarial suite that attacks the tables directly.

The operational edges are deliberately stubbed or simplified. That covers the payout [`Processor`](/economy/ports/processor/), the FX [`Rates`](/economy/ports/rates/) source, schema migrations, and concurrency at scale.

A real-world deployment would need to implement the regulated plumbing that a Money Transmitter provides: (KYC, AML & Sanctions Screening) and Payout Rails. economy-lab is the layer that sits on top: wallets, entitlements, marketplace logic, and the invariants that bind them.

It runs entirely in memory with zero runtime dependencies, and the same logic runs on Postgres, MySQL, Redis, and SQS through swappable [adapters](/economy/ports/storage-and-messaging/). What's intentionally out of scope has its own page: [scope and non-goals](/economy/scope-and-non-goals/).

## Where the ideas come from

economy-lab invents no cryptography and no accounting. It composes four well-established ideas into one ledger:

- **Double-entry bookkeeping.** The rule that every posting balances (so value can be neither minted nor lost) is the method Luca Pacioli first set down in print in 1494. It's the foundation of [accounts & double-entry](/economy/concepts/accounts-and-double-entry/).
- **Hash-linked tamper-evidence.** Hashing each record onto the one before it, so any later edit is detectable, comes from Haber and Stornetta's [_How to Time-Stamp a Digital Document_](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/BF00196791.pdf) (1991), the construction Bitcoin would later cite. The per-account [hash chain](/economy/concepts/integrity/) applies it one chain per account.
- **Merkle trees, Certificate Transparency, and Ed25519.** The signed [checkpoint](/economy/concepts/integrity/) folds every account's head into a single Merkle root and signs it. The hash tree is Ralph Merkle's, from his [1979 thesis](https://www.ralphmerkle.com/papers/Thesis1979.pdf) and [later patent](https://patents.google.com/patent/US4881264A/); the leaf/node domain separation follows [RFC 6962](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/rfc6962/) (Certificate Transparency); and the signature is [Ed25519](https://ed25519.cr.yp.to/) (Bernstein and colleagues).
- **Sagas.** A payout that reaches an outside rail is modeled as a long-lived transaction advanced one compensatable step at a time, the [saga](/economy/concepts/lifecycles/) pattern from Garcia-Molina and Salem's [_Sagas_](https://sigmodrecord.org/1987/12/09/sagas/) (SIGMOD 1987).

Binding them together (accounting conservation, cryptographic tamper-evidence, and compensatable long-lived transactions) under one [prover](/economy/concepts/the-proof/) that re-checks all of them after every operation.
