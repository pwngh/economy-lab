Two kinds of value move through the economy: in-app <Code variant="value">CREDIT</Code> and real-world <Code variant="value">USD</Code>. Each is an <ApiLink to="types/src.Amount" />: a <ApiLink to="types/src.Currency" /> plus a count of minor units held as a <Code variant="type">bigint</Code>. They convert to each other only at fixed, platform-set rates, never a live market.

Almost the whole model follows from three of those rates, ordered `buy ≥ par ≥ payout`. You pay the `buy` rate to acquire a credit. The platform backs and cashes out each credit at `par`. A seller settles earned credits at `payout`, which equals `par`.

The gap between `buy` and `par` is the platform spread, the single place the platform takes a margin.

## Amount: a currency and a scale

Every `Amount` carries its currency: the shape is `{ currency, minor, __brand }`, where `minor` is a `bigint` of minor units (cents, for `USD`) and `Currency` is the string union `'CREDIT' | 'USD'`. Minor units are exact integers, so large platform totals never lose precision the way a `number` does past `2^53`.

```ts
const price = toAmount('CREDIT', 1000n); // 10.00 CREDIT
encodeAmount(price); // → 'CREDIT:10.00'
```

A whole unit is <ApiLink to="variables/src.SCALE" /> (`100n`) minor units, two decimal places, fixed. The `__brand` field makes a plain `{ currency, minor }` object unassignable to `Amount`, so every value has to pass through <ApiLink to="functions/src.toAmount" /> or <ApiLink to="functions/src.decodeAmount" />. For the common case of a whole number of credits, <ApiLink to="functions/src.credits">credits(120)</ApiLink> is shorthand for `toAmount('CREDIT', 12_000n)`.

Two guards catch bad amounts early. <ApiLink to="functions/src.add" /> and <ApiLink to="functions/src.compare" /> call `assertSameCurrency`, which throws `CURRENCY_MISMATCH` if you hand them two different currencies — combining a `CREDIT` amount with a `USD` amount is always a bug. And `decodeAmount` rejects a string with more than two decimal places (`INVALID_AMOUNT`) rather than silently dropping the extra digits.

<Callout type="note">
  Credits are whole units in practice (a user buys, spends, and earns round
  numbers), but an `Amount` still stores them in minor units so the same
  exact-integer math covers both currencies.
</Callout>

## Why two rates exist, and where the fee lives

If a credit cashed out for exactly what you paid, the platform would earn nothing on it, so a credit costs more to buy than it pays back at cash-out. The [`Rates`](/economy/ports/rates/) port supplies three fixed CREDIT-to-USD rates from an audited source, never from caller input. They always hold the order `buy ≥ par ≥ payout`:

| Rate     | What it is                                                                                                      | Example                       |
| -------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| `buy`    | The acquisition rate: what a user pays per credit when buying.                                                  | ~120 credits / USD ($0.00833) |
| `par`    | The redemption/backing rate: a credit's cash-out floor, and the value the backing check holds real USD against. | ~200 credits / USD ($0.005)   |
| `payout` | The seller settlement rate: what an earned credit converts to USD at. Equals `par`.                            | ~200 credits / USD ($0.005)   |

The `buy`-to-`par` gap is the platform spread, about 40% on the example rates above — the platform's margin, which funds fees, payment processing, reserves, and operating costs. It is taken **once, at purchase**, and never mixed into the cash held to back users.

<RateLadder />

Walk a `$10` purchase through: it becomes `1,200` credits. Of those, `$6.00` (`1,200 × par`) is set aside as backing, and `$4.00` is the platform's to keep. And because `payout = par`, a credit a seller earns is worth exactly the dollars reserved for it.

A <ApiLink to="types/src_ports.Rate" /> is stored as exact integers (`{ rate, scale, rateId }`) with the multiplier `rate / 10^scale` (so `rate: 5n, scale: 3` is `$0.005` per credit). The `rateId` names the exact rate. That lets a [transaction](/economy/concepts/accounts-and-double-entry/) record which rate it used, and lets a [payout saga](/economy/concepts/payout-saga/) lock to one. Conversion rounds in the safe direction per call site: payouts and the solvency check round down (<ApiLink to="functions/src.convertFloor" />), while a top-up rounds its gross and backing up (<ApiLink to="functions/src.convertCeil" />) so held cash always covers the solvency valuation.

## The invariant: buy ≥ par ≥ payout

`buy ≥ par` is what keeps the platform's margin non-negative. A top-up values the buyer's cash at `buy` and the backing held in trust at `par`. The difference is recognized as USD revenue, added as a posting line only when positive. If `buy` ever dropped below `par`, that difference would go negative and a purchase would book a loss.

The reference [`configuredRates`](/economy/ports/rates/) adapter takes `buy`, `par`, and `payout` as separate deployment constants and checks the ordering at construction: a miswired deployment throws `CONFIG_INVALID` before it can book a loss. The economy itself re-checks `buy ≥ par` when it is built and again inside each top-up, so even a custom rate source fails by name rather than booking a loss.

`par ≥ payout` (in practice `payout = par`) ties the seller's cash-out — the creator payout, in marketplace terms — to the same value the platform reserved. That's what [solvency](/economy/concepts/solvency/) checks: real USD in trust covers every spendable credit valued at `par`. It is enforced where the payout is priced: `requestPayout` refuses a payout rate above `par` with `CONFIG_INVALID`, and the USD quote it stores is exactly what the rail is paid and the ledger posts.

## How the spread is enforced

There is no "purchase fee" anywhere in the code. The spread is realized by the split at top-up. Because cash is valued at `buy` going in and backing at `par`, the margin just falls out of `buy − par`.

The marketplace **fee** on a sale sits outside the money model entirely. How a sale's price splits into recipient shares and platform revenue is the [pricing policy](/economy/ports/pricing/)'s job. You can see the balanced <ApiLink to="types/src_ports.Leg" />s it produces in [accounts and double-entry](/economy/concepts/accounts-and-double-entry/).

## What relies on it

- [Accounts and double-entry](/economy/concepts/accounts-and-double-entry/): every posting is a balanced set of `Leg`s whose amounts net to zero in each currency. The `Amount` guards keep a posting from mixing currencies on one line.
- [Solvency](/economy/concepts/solvency/): the backing check values spendable credits in USD at `par` to confirm trust cash covers them.
- The [`Rates`](/economy/ports/rates/) port supplies `buy`/`par`/`payout`; top-up and the payout saga read it.
- The [`FeePolicy`](/economy/ports/pricing/) port: the marketplace fee and recipient split, the only withholding outside the buy-par spread.
